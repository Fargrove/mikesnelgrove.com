Workshop Recap

Is AI Helping or Hurting?

AI is helping when... AI is hurting when...
They use it to explain a concept they don't understand They use it to write something they could write themselves
They generate practice questions to study with They generate the assignment itself
They brainstorm ideas, then do their own thinking They copy-paste and change a few words
They use it to break a big task into smaller steps They have AI do each step instead of just planning them
An ESL student uses it to translate concepts for family They rely on it so heavily they stop building their own skills
They can explain their work without the AI They couldn't redo the work if you took the AI away
"Could you do this without the AI?"
If yes — they're using it as a tool.   If no — the AI is doing their thinking for them.

5 Conversation Starters

  1. "What's the weirdest thing you've seen AI get wrong?"
  2. "Do your teachers talk about AI? What do they say?"
  3. "If you could use AI for anything with no rules, what would you use it for?"
  4. "Do you think AI makes school easier or just different?"
  5. "Has AI ever given you advice that felt off?"

3 Things to Try This Week

1. Have the "Show Me" conversation. "I went to this thing at John Oliver about AI and I'm curious. Can you show me how you use it for homework? I'm not trying to catch you. I genuinely want to see."

2. Use AI yourself, in front of your child. Try it for something real (drafting an email, looking up a recipe, researching a purchase). Then question the result out loud. When they see you use it AND question it, both become normal.

3. Ask the one question. Next time your child finishes an assignment, ask casually: "Could you do that without the AI?" No judgment. Just curiosity.

When Things Go Wrong

Three common situations you might face, with conversation starting points. There's no single right way to handle these. Use what feels natural to you and your family.

You discover your child submitted an AI-written essay

This is probably the most common scenario parents will encounter. The essay reads well, maybe better than your child's usual writing, but something feels off. Or a teacher flags it.

A way in: "I noticed your essay reads really differently from your usual writing. Can you walk me through how you wrote it? I'm curious about your process."

If they used AI, the follow-up question is: "Could you have written this without it?" Listen to their answer. The goal is to understand their habits, not to punish a single incident.

Your child's grades have suddenly improved and you're not sure why

This is not necessarily a problem. Better grades could mean they're studying more effectively, or it could mean AI is doing the heavy lifting. The university wake-up story from the workshop is relevant here: students getting 95% on AI-assisted homework were scoring 40% on exams.

A way in: "Your marks have been really strong lately. What's changed? I'd love to know what's working."

If AI comes up, follow with the "Show Me" approach: "Can you show me how you're using it? I genuinely want to see." You'll learn more from watching than from asking.

You find your child talking to an AI companion late at night

This one is less about school and more about emotional well-being. AI companions are designed to validate. They agree with everything. They never push back, never challenge, never say "I think you're wrong." For a teenager, that can feel comforting but it's not the same as a real relationship.

A way in: "I saw you were up late on your phone. Everything okay? I'm not checking up on you, I just want to make sure you're doing alright."

The real question underneath is whether your child has enough human connection. If they're turning to AI for emotional support, it's worth thinking about why, not banning the tool.

How AI Actually Works

A plain-language explanation you can share with your family.

When you text "Happy" on your phone, it suggests "Birthday." It does this because it has learned the pattern: millions of people have typed "Happy Birthday" before you.

AI chatbots like ChatGPT and Claude work the same way, just at a much bigger scale. They've been trained on most of the text on the internet and have learned patterns for how words follow each other. When your child types a question, the AI doesn't "know" the answer. It predicts the most likely next word, over and over, until it has a paragraph.

The short version: AI is autocomplete on a massive scale. It sounds confident. It can be very useful. But it has no idea whether what it's saying is true.

What is a "hallucination"? This is the term for when AI states something completely made up as if it were fact. No hedging, no "I'm not sure." Just a confident, detailed answer that happens to be fiction. In 2023, a lawyer in New York submitted a legal brief containing six case citations that AI had invented. Fake case names, fake judges, fake rulings. The lawyer was sanctioned by the court.

Why this matters for your child: If your child is using AI for homework and the AI gives them a quote, a date, or a source, it might be real or it might be completely invented. They have no way to know without checking. The first skill your child needs is the habit of verifying what AI tells them.
